## The top-K primitive

You've embedded your chunks and stored them in a vector index. A query arrives. Now what?

The answer is almost always some version of the same thing: **rank every chunk by its similarity to the query, then take the K highest-ranked ones.** That's top-K retrieval. Everything else in this chapter is a variation on or reaction to this primitive.

The similarity function is typically cosine similarity — the same measure from the Embeddings chapter. The query is embedded using the same model as the corpus, giving it a point in the same vector space. Then you compute the angle between the query vector and every document vector. The K chunks with the smallest angles (highest cosine similarity) win.

A few things this approach gets right: it's fast with an approximate nearest-neighbor index, it generalizes across query types, and the score is interpretable — you always have a number you can inspect. What it gets wrong comes next.

At K=1 or K=2, the retrieved set is tight: just the documents that clearly mention Paris or France. At K=4 or K=5, documents about Tokyo, sushi, or programming languages start slipping in. Their similarity scores are non-zero — the word "the" appears in every document, which creates a small baseline similarity even for completely off-topic chunks.

This is the precision-recall tradeoff in its most naked form. **Small K gives you high precision but risks missing something relevant.** If a query needs two pieces of information and you only fetch one, your LLM can't synthesize an answer from what it never saw. **Large K gives you more recall but introduces noise.** A 4096-token context window fills up fast; chunks that don't belong push out chunks that do.

The right K is not 3 or 5 or 10. It depends on your query distribution, your chunk size, and how densely your corpus covers the topic. Measure on real queries.

(Note: this sim uses toy bag-of-words cosine similarity, not real embeddings. Real embeddings would push the Paris/Eiffel/Louvre documents closer together and Tokyo/Python further apart, making the cutoff cleaner — but the shape of the problem is the same.)

## The redundancy problem

Top-K assumes that the K most similar documents are the K most *useful* documents. That assumption breaks down as soon as your corpus contains near-duplicate content.

Imagine a knowledge base about a product where the same FAQ answer appears verbatim in three places: the main FAQ page, a troubleshooting guide, and a getting-started document. A user asks a question that matches that FAQ. Under pure top-K retrieval, your K=3 result set is three copies of the same answer. You've used your entire retrieval budget on one piece of information.

This isn't hypothetical. It happens whenever you have:

- Product documentation with version-specific copies of the same page
- News articles reporting the same event with slight paraphrasing
- Customer support corpora where the same resolution is pasted into multiple tickets
- Any corpus that grew organically and was never deduplicated

The result is that your LLM sees K copies of fact A and zero copies of facts B, C, and D — even though B, C, and D might be exactly what completes the answer. **Pure relevance ranking can silently waste your retrieval budget on redundancy.**

## MMR — relevance vs diversity

Maximal Marginal Relevance (MMR) is a greedy algorithm that reranks documents to balance relevance against redundancy. The score for each candidate document at each selection step is:

```
MMR_score = λ · sim(doc, query) − (1 − λ) · max_{selected} sim(doc, selected_doc)
```

The first term rewards relevance to the query. The second term penalizes similarity to documents you've already selected. Lambda (λ) controls the balance: λ=1 reduces MMR to vanilla top-K; λ=0 selects the most diverse set possible regardless of relevance.

Before running the sim, think about what λ=1 should produce.

>
  <MMRRetriever initialLambda={0.5} />
</PredictGate>

At λ=1, MMR and vanilla top-K select identical documents — confirming the formula works as described. As you drag λ toward 0, documents diverge: MMR starts swapping in lower-relevance-but-different documents to avoid repetition.

The interesting region is the middle. At λ=0.5, MMR balances relevance and diversity. For this toy corpus and query, it may swap the third Paris-adjacent document for something topically distinct — say, a document about Tokyo — because the redundancy penalty outweighs the marginal relevance gain.

**Lambda is a hyperparameter, not a constant.** The right value depends on your use case:

- Queries that need confirmation ("Is the capital of France Paris?") benefit from high lambda — you want the most relevant result, not variety.
- Queries that need breadth ("What should I know about Paris?") benefit from lower lambda — you want distinct angles, not three restatements of the same fact.
- Factoid QA systems often stay near λ=0.7–0.9. Research summarization pipelines often drop to λ=0.4–0.6.

Like chunk size, lambda is something you tune against real query logs, not something you set once and forget.
