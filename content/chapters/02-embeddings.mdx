## What is an embedding?

An embedding is a fixed-length list of numbers — a vector — that encodes the *meaning* of a piece of text. Feed a model the word "king" and it returns something like `[0.12, 0.88, ...]`. Feed it "queen" and you get a different vector, but one that is numerically close to the first. Feed it "pizza" and you get a vector far away from both.

This is the core mechanism behind semantic retrieval. Instead of asking "does this document contain the word 'monarch'?", a RAG system asks "is this document's vector close to the query's vector?" That shift — from matching symbols to measuring geometric distance — is what lets retrieval survive synonym substitution, paraphrase, and translation.

The model that produces embeddings is called an **encoder**. It's a separate model from the generator (the LLM that produces answers). You run the encoder once at index time for every chunk, store the resulting vectors in a vector database, and then run it again at query time to embed the incoming question. Retrieval is the geometry problem that follows.

One detail worth fixing early: an embedding is not a "summary" and it is not human-readable. It is a coordinate in a high-dimensional space. What makes it useful is not any single dimension — it's the position *relative to other vectors* that carries meaning.

## Measuring similarity

"Close" needs a precise definition. The standard one in embedding retrieval is **cosine similarity**: the cosine of the angle between two vectors.

```
cosine(A, B) = (A · B) / (|A| × |B|)
```

The dot product in the numerator measures directional alignment. Dividing by the magnitudes normalizes away vector length, so a short vector and a long vector pointing in the same direction score 1.0. Vectors pointing in opposite directions score -1.0. Perpendicular vectors score 0.

This matters because embedding magnitude is not meaningful — two embeddings of the same sentence produced by the same model will have the same direction but potentially different magnitudes depending on implementation details. Cosine similarity is robust to that noise.

## Semantic vs lexical

Lexical similarity counts shared words. Semantic similarity measures shared meaning. These come apart constantly, and the gap is why keyword search fails at retrieval tasks where RAG is supposed to shine.

Consider two pairs:

**High lexical overlap, low semantic similarity:** "The bank approved my loan" and "The river bank flooded last spring." Both sentences contain "bank." A keyword search treats them as related. Their embedding vectors point in very different directions, because encoders learn from context — "bank" next to "loan" and "approved" patterns like a financial institution; "bank" next to "river" and "flooded" patterns like a geographic feature. The model has seen thousands of examples of each usage and has separated them in its internal space.

**Low lexical overlap, high semantic similarity:** "The king reigned for forty years" and "The monarch ruled the realm." No words in common besides function words. A keyword search gives them near-zero overlap. Their embeddings are close — because "king" and "monarch" appear in nearly identical contexts in training data, and so do "reigned" and "ruled." The cosine similarity score will be high.

The SimilarityScorer demonstrated a toy version of this: synonym swaps within a cluster keep the score high; jumping to a different cluster tanks it. Real encoders generalize this to the entire vocabulary and across languages.

This is the reason RAG systems use vector search rather than BM25 or simple keyword matching: a user who asks "how do I revoke someone's access?" should retrieve a chunk that says "to remove permissions, navigate to..." — no shared content words, but high semantic similarity. And a chunk about "bank transactions" should *not* surface just because the word "access" appeared in a description of an ATM.

One nuance worth tracking: semantic similarity is about *sentence-level meaning in context*, not just word proximity. The embedding of a full chunk reflects all of its words jointly — which is why chunk size, from the previous chapter, still matters. A chunk that mixes two unrelated topics produces an embedding that sits ambiguously between them, scoring mediocre for both. Clean, focused chunks produce embeddings that sit cleanly in one region of the space — which is exactly what you want for high-precision retrieval.

## In practice

The previous sections gave you the mechanism. This one tells you what working
engineers actually pick, why model choice is less important than people think,
and where the textbook story silently fails in production.

### Which embedding model do real systems use?

As of 2025–2026, the shortlist most teams start from:

- **OpenAI `text-embedding-3-small`**: 1536 dimensions, $0.02/1M tokens. The
  cheap default for OpenAI shops. Good quality-per-dollar; works well on English
  prose and code.
- **OpenAI `text-embedding-3-large`**: 3072 dimensions, $0.13/1M tokens. Better
  on hard retrieval benchmarks but rarely worth 6× the cost in production — the
  gap closes fast when you tune chunking and query rewriting.
- **Cohere `embed-english-v3`** and **`embed-multilingual-v3`**: 1024 dimensions.
  Competitive on the MTEB leaderboard; multilingual variant is the default choice
  when your corpus mixes languages.
- **Voyage `voyage-2`**: 1024 dimensions. Consistently high on MTEB for code and
  retrieval tasks; worth evaluating if OpenAI's quality is borderline for your corpus.
- **Open-source: `BAAI/bge-large-en-v1.5`** and **`intfloat/e5-large-v2`**: both
  768 dimensions, run locally. BGE and E5 regularly appear at the top of MTEB and
  are the go-to choices for on-premise deployments or when you need to avoid API
  egress costs.

The [MTEB leaderboard](https://huggingface.co/spaces/mteb/leaderboard) is the
canonical benchmark. Use it to form a shortlist — but do not treat leaderboard
rank as your answer. MTEB aggregates over many tasks and domains. A model that
ranks third overall may outrank first place on your specific corpus. Always run a
small retrieval eval on 50–100 of your real queries before committing to a model.

### Dimensions matter and they don't

More dimensions means larger vectors: more storage, slower ANN search, higher
memory footprint. The quality ceiling rises too — but it rises steeply up to
~512–768 dimensions and then flattens. Going from 256 to 768 dimensions typically
produces a meaningful quality jump. Going from 768 to 1536 rarely does, and going
from 1536 to 3072 almost never does on real RAG workloads.

**Matryoshka embeddings** change this calculus. OpenAI's `text-embedding-3-large`
and Nomic's `nomic-embed-text-v1.5` are trained with Matryoshka Representation
Learning (MRL), which means you can truncate the vector to 256, 512, or 768
dimensions and still get most of the quality. OpenAI reports that truncating
`text-embedding-3-large` to 256 dimensions loses only ~6% of retrieval quality
compared to the full 3072-dim vector — while cutting storage by 12×.

In practice, production systems often run **512–768 dimensions** as the cost-quality
sweet spot. If you're using a Matryoshka model, start at 512 and benchmark before
paying for more dimensions. If you're on a fixed model without MRL, 1024–1536 is
the practical ceiling worth paying for.

### Embedding drift

When a vendor updates their encoder, old and new vectors live in incompatible geometric spaces — a query from the new model finds nonsense in the old index.

<EmbeddingDriftViz />

The correct migration is to either re-embed everything before switching the query encoder, or run parallel indexes during the transition. The key operational discipline: **log the model name with every embedding at write time** so you can detect model mismatches before they silently destroy retrieval quality.

### Same encoder for queries AND chunks

This sounds obvious but is violated constantly. If you embed chunks with model A
and queries with model B, the vectors live in different spaces and cosine similarity
between them is meaningless. Retrieval will appear to work (the search returns
results) but the results will be arbitrary.

The failure mode in practice: a team prototypes with `bge-large` for indexing,
then switches to the OpenAI API for query embedding because it's "easier to call"
and they "just want to test the LLM side." Retrieval tanks and they spend two days
blaming prompt engineering. The fix: enforce at the application level that the same
model name is used for both index writes and query embedding. The logging discipline
described above (storing model name with every vector) catches this class of bug
automatically.

### Asymmetric vs symmetric search

Some encoder models — specifically the E5 family (`intfloat/e5-large-v2`,
`intfloat/e5-mistral-7b-instruct`) and the BGE family (`BAAI/bge-large-en-v1.5`) —
require instruction prefixes:

```
Query embedding:   "query: How do I reset my password?"
Passage embedding: "passage: To reset your password, navigate to Settings..."
```

Forgetting these prefixes degrades retrieval quality by **10–15%** on standard
benchmarks. This is documented in each model's card; the fix is one line of code.
OpenAI and Cohere models do not require this — their asymmetry is baked into the
training objective without user-visible prefixes. Check the model card before you
trust a benchmark. If you're seeing unexpectedly poor performance from an open-source
model, missing prefixes are the first thing to check.

### The single highest-leverage embedding trick

Don't embed the raw chunk. Embed the **header chain + chunk**. If a chunk came from
a Markdown document with headers `## Setup → ### Database`, prepend that chain so
the embedding reads:

```
## Setup / ### Database / [chunk text here]
```

This is the embedding-specific application of the broader contextual retrieval
technique covered in Chapter 1's "In practice" section — Anthropic's contextual
retrieval paper reported retrieval-failure reductions of 35–67% from situating
chunks in their document context. For embedding specifically, the mechanism is
direct: the vector now encodes *where the chunk lives* as well as *what the chunk
says*. A chunk that reads "click Save" is ambiguous in isolation; a chunk that reads
"Setup / Database / click Save" is not.

You don't need an LLM call for this. Static header prepending costs a few tokens per
chunk at index time and is two lines of code. If you have an LLM-generated prefix
available (from contextual retrieval), use that instead — it's better. But static
headers beat raw chunks almost universally.

### The failure mode you will see in production

> "Retrieval was great on the test set. After launch, every question that uses a product name, internal abbreviation, or team-specific jargon comes back empty."

Root cause: **cosine similarity is anchored in training distribution**. Out-of-domain terminology lowers similarity scores even when the semantic intent is identical.

<OutOfDistributionFailure />

The eval to catch this before launch: include queries written by users (or proxies for users) in your offline eval set, not just queries written by the documentation authors. A retrieval@5 of 92% on author-written queries can coexist with 40% on user-written queries for the same corpus.

### Where embeddings fit in the bigger system

The encoder bridges two sides of the pipeline — indexing chunks at write time, then embedding the query at request time through the same model.

<EmbeddingPipelineFlow />

The choice of encoder model determines the geometry of the space the ANN search operates in. Everything downstream — what "nearest" means, which chunks surface — follows from that geometry. This is why encoder choice is a load-bearing architectural decision: you can change chunk size post-launch with a re-index, but changing encoder model requires re-embedding the entire corpus. Pick deliberately.

### Root cause: vectors from two incompatible spaces

The most likely root cause is a partial migration — the query encoder was switched to `text-embedding-3-small`, but the chunk vectors in the index were never re-embedded. The `EmbeddingDriftViz` in this chapter makes this visible: the same words cluster in completely different positions under `ada-002` vs `text-embedding-3-small`. The cosine similarity between a `3-small` query vector and an `ada-002` chunk vector is geometrically meaningless — it's measuring the angle between two points that live in different coordinate systems.

The symptom fits perfectly. Precision@5 = 87% with the old model (both query and chunks in the same space) collapsing to 31% after migration (query in new space, chunks in old space) is the canonical embedding drift signature. Results don't go to zero because some dimensions happen to overlap by chance, but they're effectively random.

### Confirming the diagnosis

1. **Check stored model metadata.** If you've been logging the model name with every vector (as described in this chapter's "Embedding drift" sub-section), query your vector DB for a sample of chunk vectors and check their `encoder_model` metadata field. If it shows `text-embedding-ada-002` while your query config now uses `text-embedding-3-small`, you have your answer in 30 seconds.

2. **Spot-check embedding similarity.** Take a query you know should retrieve a specific chunk. Embed the query with `3-small`. Pull the chunk's stored vector. Compute cosine similarity directly. A score near zero (or negative) confirms cross-space contamination.

3. **Verify the query config.** Confirm which model is currently being called for query embedding. A common bug: the migration updated the indexing config but left the query config pointing at the old model, or vice versa.

### Recovery plan (no write-freeze)

**Option 1 — Re-embed in parallel (recommended):** Stand up a background job that re-embeds all chunks with `text-embedding-3-small` and writes them to a new index namespace. Keep serving queries from the old namespace (with the old encoder) while the background job runs. Once re-indexing is complete, flip the query encoder and the namespace in a single atomic config change. Zero downtime, clean cutover. Cost: one-time re-embedding of the full corpus.

**Option 2 — Run dual indexes:** Route every query to both the old index (with old encoder) and the new index (with new encoder). Merge results by rank. This gets you partial quality improvement immediately while the new index fills up, but adds latency and complexity. Worth it only if you can't afford the downtime of Option 1's cutover moment.

**Option 3 — Roll back immediately:** Switch the query encoder back to `ada-002` to restore the 87% precision while you plan the proper migration. This is the fastest recovery if the business impact of 31% precision is acute.

### Why this is the canonical answer

This is the exact failure mode the `EmbeddingDriftViz` in this chapter illustrates: two models produce different vector space geometries, and mixing them produces nonsense retrieval. The "Embedding drift" sub-section explicitly calls out the migration options — re-embed everything, or run parallel indexes. The logging discipline (store model name with every vector) is the diagnostic lever that lets you confirm this bug in 30 seconds vs. days of guessing. In interviews, naming the root cause (partial migration, cross-space contamination) and one concrete recovery path that avoids a write-freeze is the senior-level answer.

### Storage math

At float32 (4 bytes per dimension), the three options with 50M chunks:

- **256-dim:** ~51 GB → ~$2.6/month at $0.05/GB. Well within the $500 budget, leaves headroom for metadata and HNSW graph overhead (typically 1.5–2× the raw vector size).
- **768-dim:** ~154 GB → ~$7.7/month. Still well within budget. This is the sweet spot the `DimensionTradeoffChart` in this chapter illustrates — quality rises steeply to ~768 dims and then plateaus.
- **1536-dim:** ~307 GB → ~$15.4/month. Well within the $500 budget too, but the quality gain over 768-dim is marginal for most RAG workloads. The HNSW index grows roughly proportionally, adding latency.

All three options fit the storage budget comfortably. Storage is not the deciding constraint at this scale.

### Latency math

ANN search latency scales with dimensionality (dot product computation per candidate) and index size. At 50M vectors:

- **256-dim:** Fastest. p95 latency on a well-provisioned HNSW index: ~30–50ms for the ANN step. Leaves ~100ms for embedding + LLM overhead.
- **768-dim:** ~2–3× more computation per dot product than 256-dim, but HNSW pruning means you don't compute all 50M. Realistic p95: ~50–80ms for ANN. Tight but likely within the 150ms p95 budget if embedding latency is low.
- **1536-dim:** ~6× more computation than 256-dim. p95: ~80–120ms for ANN on 50M vectors. This puts the 150ms p95 target at risk unless you accept lower HNSW recall (fewer candidates checked) or add hardware.

### Quality reality

The `DimensionTradeoffChart` shows the quality plateau. The empirical data point: OpenAI reports that Matryoshka-truncated `text-embedding-3-large` at 256 dimensions retains ~94% of full-dim quality. Going from 256 to 768 dims typically closes that gap significantly. Going from 768 to 1536 dims gains ~1–3% on standard benchmarks — rarely worth the latency and complexity cost for RAG workloads.

### Recommendation

**Start with 768-dim BGE (`bge-large-en-v1.5`)**: open-source (zero API cost), 768-dim sweet spot, consistently top-tier on MTEB retrieval tasks. Storage is ~$7.7/month. ANN latency fits the 150ms budget. If you need to go bigger, use a Matryoshka model and benchmark at 512 before paying for more dimensions.

**Change the recommendation if:**
- Your corpus is multilingual → switch to Cohere `embed-multilingual-v3` (1024-dim, API cost but best multilingual quality).
- After running retrieval evals on your real queries, 768-dim BGE falls short → try OpenAI `text-embedding-3-small` at 1536-dim and check if the latency budget still clears.
- You need to reduce latency below 100ms p95 → consider 256-dim Matryoshka truncation and measure the quality hit on your actual corpus.

### Why this is the canonical answer

The `DimensionTradeoffChart` in this chapter's In Practice section makes the quality plateau visible — 256→768 is a real gain; 768→1536 is marginal. The quantified storage numbers ($2.6–$15.4/month) and the latency framing (all three options fit the budget but 1536-dim puts p95 at risk) are what distinguish a senior answer from "more dimensions = better." The recommendation starts with the open-source sweet spot and gives explicit switching conditions — which is how real architectural decisions work.

### Root cause: out-of-distribution query terminology

The eval set was written by the same people who wrote the documentation — they used "SSO" and "SAML" just like the docs do. Real users call the same thing by different names: "the SSO thing," "work credentials," "company login," "that auth thing IT set up." The metric that hid this failure is **retrieval@5 evaluated on author-written queries**. This metric measures whether any relevant chunk appears in the top-5 — but it only catches failures your eval queries trigger. User queries using "work login thing" never appear in the eval set, so the failure is invisible.

**1. Query rewriting (highest return on effort).** Before embedding the user's query, use an LLM to normalize it to canonical terminology: "the work login thing" → "SSO configuration and SAML setup." The `OutOfDistributionFailure` sim labels this the first mitigation chip — it's first because it's the fastest to ship.

**2. Synonym expansion in metadata.** Store known aliases for key terms in chunk metadata (e.g., `aliases: ["SSO", "SAML", "single sign-on", "work login"]`) and run a hybrid search: vector search to find semantically nearby docs, BM25 or keyword filter to also surface docs whose metadata aliases match the query's literal tokens. This catches the cases where query rewriting doesn't normalize perfectly.

### Is the math correct?

Yes — exactly. When both vectors are unit-normalized, `A · B = |A| × |B| × cos(θ) = 1 × 1 × cos(θ) = cos(θ)`. Dot product and cosine similarity become identical. This is a well-established optimization; Pinecone, Weaviate, and most ANN libraries apply it internally. The reviewer understands the math correctly.

The formula from this chapter's "Measuring similarity" section: `cosine(A, B) = (A · B) / (|A| × |B|)`. Pre-normalizing eliminates the division at query time. For 50M vectors at 1536 dimensions, this is a real speedup — not dramatic, but real.

### PR review comment

---

**Approve with minor flag.**

The math is correct — dot product on unit-normalized vectors is identical to cosine similarity, and the per-query speedup is real. Most managed vector databases (Pinecone, Weaviate, Qdrant) do this normalization internally anyway, so you may be duplicating work that already happens. Worth checking the client library docs before you add the normalization step.

**What I'd approve immediately:**
- Normalizing vectors before upsert if your vector DB doesn't do it internally.
- The comment in the code explaining why — future engineers shouldn't have to re-derive this.

**What I'd flag:**
- If you ever want to use **dot-product similarity without normalization** (e.g., to weight results by embedding magnitude as a confidence proxy), you lose that option permanently. Low-magnitude embeddings — which some models produce for short, ambiguous queries — carry a signal that normalization discards.
- **Verify that your vector DB isn't already normalizing.** Applying unit normalization twice is harmless but confusing.
- Store the original L2 norm in metadata if you want to be able to recover it. Cheap to store, hard to recompute at query time.

---

### The scenario where this hurts retrieval

**Maximum Inner Product Search (MIPS) for recommendation systems.** Some retrieval setups deliberately use non-normalized dot product to surface items where high embedding magnitude encodes popularity or confidence. A short, ambiguous query like "settings" has a low-magnitude embedding; a specific well-formed query like "how do I configure SAML SSO for Okta" has a higher magnitude. If your ANN index uses raw dot product, the magnitude difference can help rank specificity. After normalization, both queries have the same magnitude = 1, and that signal disappears. This doesn't affect standard RAG (where cosine is correct), but if your architecture ever evolves toward a hybrid retrieval mode that exploits magnitude, you've lost the option.

### Why this is the canonical answer

The cosine similarity formula in this chapter's "Measuring similarity" section makes the equivalence explicit: dividing by `|A| × |B|` normalizes out magnitude. The senior review doesn't just say "yes, approve" — it checks whether the DB already does this, flags the MIPS edge case, and proposes preserving the original norm as metadata. ### Why 200K context doesn't scale: the numbers

**Cost.** GPT-4 Turbo at $0.01/1K input tokens. Stuffing 50M tokens per query: **$500 per query**. At 1,000 queries/day, that's $500,000/day. RAG retrieves 2K–8K tokens per query: **$0.02–$0.08 per query** — a 6,000–25,000× cost reduction. The math is not close.

Even if you pre-process the 50M tokens into 250 chunks of 200K and cache them with prompt caching (~90% discount), you're at ~$12,500/day. Still 625× more expensive than RAG at the same query volume.

**Latency.** GPT-4 takes 10–30 seconds to process and generate over a 200K context. RAG adds ~70–150ms for embedding + ANN search, then runs the LLM over 2K–8K tokens (~1–3 seconds). The user-facing latency difference is 5–10× in RAG's favor.

**Retrieval quality.** Larger context windows have "lost in the middle" problems — models pay disproportionately more attention to content at the beginning and end of a long context, and may miss or misweight a relevant fact buried at position 80K in a 200K prompt. RAG retrieves the most relevant chunks and presents them at the top of a short context, where the model attends to them reliably.

### The one scenario where the CTO is right

**When your total corpus fits in a single reasonably-sized context window and you query it frequently with context-dependent questions.** Example: a 40-page technical spec where every question depends on definitions from section 1 to interpret clauses in section 8. RAG would retrieve the wrong chunks because the query doesn't contain the right keywords to pull in the cross-referenced definitions. Stuffing the whole spec — if it fits in 128K tokens at ~$1.28 per query — may actually produce better answers than retrieval. For a low-volume internal tool with a small, stable document set, this is a legitimate choice.

The threshold: if your corpus is under ~100K tokens and your queries routinely require whole-document context (legal analysis, contract review, multi-section technical specs with heavy cross-references), full-context beats RAG on quality and may be acceptable on cost. Above that threshold, RAG is almost always the right default.

### Framing the answer for the CTO

The 200K window is a great tool for small, coherent documents. For 50M tokens of documentation, it's economically and latency-prohibitive by 3–5 orders of magnitude. RAG is the solution specifically for when the corpus is larger than what you can fit in a prompt — which is the common case. The architecture should match the corpus size, not ignore it.

### Why this is the canonical answer

This chapter's "Where embeddings fit in the bigger system" section and the `EmbeddingPipelineFlow` diagram establish that the encoder fits in a larger system that exists precisely because we can't fit the whole corpus in a prompt. The numbers — $500/query vs $0.02–$0.08/query, 30s vs 2s — are the grounding the CTO needs. The exception (small, coherent, cross-referenced corpus) shows you understand when RAG is the wrong tool, which is what distinguishes senior architectural judgment from cargo-cult RAG advocacy.
